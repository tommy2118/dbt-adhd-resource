Overview of DBT

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is an evidence-based approach that combines cognitive-behavioral techniques with mindfulness practices. It was originally developed to treat borderline personality disorder but has proven effective for a wide range of emotional and behavioral challenges, including depression, anxiety, ADHD, substance use, and eating disorders.

The term "dialectical" refers to the integration of two opposing concepts: acceptance and change. DBT teaches you to accept yourself, your emotions, and your circumstances while simultaneously working toward positive change and growth.

Key Takeaway

DBT combines acceptance and change strategies to help you create a life worth living by building skills in four key areas that work together as an integrated whole. With regular practice, these skills become powerful tools for managing emotions, improving relationships, and enhancing overall quality of life.

How to Use This Guide

This comprehensive guide is designed for both beginners and those looking to deepen their DBT practice. Here's how to make the most of it:

  • Start with the Mindfulness module, as it forms the foundation for all other DBT skills
  • Explore each module at your own pace, practicing skills as you learn them
  • Use the practical tools and worksheets to apply DBT skills to your daily life
  • Return to this guide as needed to refresh your understanding

Key Takeaway

Learning DBT is a journey, not a destination. Be patient with yourself and celebrate your progress along the way.

Core Modules

DBT is structured around four core skill modules that work together to create a comprehensive approach to emotional wellness and personal growth.

Mindfulness

The foundation of all DBT skills. Learn to observe, describe, and participate in the present moment with nonjudgmental awareness through 'What' skills (Observe, Describe, Participate) and 'How' skills (Nonjudgmentally, One-mindfully, Effectively).

Key skills: Wise Mind, Observe without judgment, Describe with facts
Explore Mindfulness →

Distress Tolerance

Develop skills to survive crisis situations without making them worse. Learn to accept reality as it is and find ways to tolerate pain when it cannot be immediately changed through crisis survival strategies and reality acceptance.

Key skills: TIPP, STOP, Radical Acceptance, Self-Soothe
Explore Distress Tolerance →

Emotion Regulation

Learn to understand emotions, reduce emotional vulnerability, and decrease emotional suffering. Develop skills to identify, experience, and express emotions effectively while reducing their intensity when needed.

Key skills: Check the Facts, Opposite Action, PLEASE, Building Positive Experiences
Explore Emotion Regulation →

Interpersonal Effectiveness

Develop skills to navigate relationships, set boundaries, and communicate assertively while maintaining self-respect and preserving important relationships. Learn to balance priorities, relationships, and self-respect.

Key skills: DEAR MAN, GIVE, FAST, Validating Others

Thinking Traps

Thinking traps (cognitive distortions) are patterns of thought that can distort our perception of reality and contribute to emotional distress. Recognizing and addressing these patterns is an important complement to DBT skills.

Perception Distortions

Patterns that affect how we interpret information and events, including all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, and mental filtering.

Learn More →

Expectation Traps

Patterns related to expectations we place on ourselves and others, including should statements, fairness fallacy, and mind reading.

Learn More →

Key Takeaway

Thinking traps can significantly impact our emotions and behaviors. By identifying these patterns, we can use DBT skills to challenge and modify them, leading to more balanced thinking.

Getting Started with DBT

Ready to begin your DBT journey? Here are some recommended first steps:

  1. Start with mindfulness - Begin with the foundational skills that will support all other aspects of DBT
  2. Practice one skill at a time - Master each skill before moving to the next to avoid feeling overwhelmed
  3. Create a practice routine - Set aside regular time each day for skill practice, even if just for a few minutes
  4. Track your progress - Notice improvements and celebrate small victories along the way
  5. Be patient with yourself - Learning DBT skills takes time and consistent practice
